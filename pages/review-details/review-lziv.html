Led Zeppelin

Everyone talks about the mystic symbols and the radio dominance of "Stairway," but looking back, this album is basically a high-stakes heist. Jimmy Page and the crew retreated to the countryside and essentially looted the history of blues and folk to build a Frankenstein monster. The brilliance isn't just the heaviness; it’s the weird acoustic texture of the Headley Grange hallway that makes "When the Levee Breaks" sound like the apocalypse. However, the album isn’t bulletproof. You have to admit that "Four Sticks" is a hypnotic but ultimately repetitive slog that kills the momentum of side two, and the band’s habit of "borrowing" songwriting credits (hello, Memphis Minnie) leaves a sour aftertaste on the otherwise pristine legacy of the record. It’s a masterpiece of atmosphere, sure, but it’s also a masterpiece of ego.

Released: November 8, 1971.

Jimmy Page (guitar/producer),
Robert Plant (vocals), John Paul Jones (bass/keys), Ian Stewart(keys),
